This doesn’t necessarily mean you need a house with a big yard, though it certainly helps. You can accomplish much with a long walk or time at the park, and toy and miniature Eskimos can be taxed by vigorous play sessions at your apartment.
Don’t neglect mental stimulation either. These are super-smart dogs, and they need their brains to be challenged as much as their legs. Marathon training sessions and puzzle toys are great ways to tucker out their intellect.
The fact that they’re athletic and intelligent makes them a natural for things like agility competitions, so you may want to think about getting your pup involved.
Their intelligence and natural eagerness to please make them easy to train, as they’ll soak up any instruction that you can give them. You should start training them from the first day that you bring them home and continue to do so throughout their lives.
Being so smart also means they get bored easily. You can’t just put them through the same paces every time, or else their focus will start to wander, and the sessions will lose their effectiveness. Try to mix things up so they’re constantly challenged and stimulated.
They respond best to positive reinforcement, and if you use punishment or other punitive measures, they may simply learn not to trust you. A kind word and a scratch behind the ears are all you need to keep them motivated, so don’t feel like you have to keep dumping treats down their throat to get them to behave.
If possible, try to incorporate your entire family into the training process, as these dogs have a tendency to glom onto the person giving the instruction. This could cause them to become possessive of that person, which will create a whole new issue for you to address in your sessions.
One of the most distinctive things about the American Eskimo is their beautiful, immaculate white coat. Here’s a secret, though: Like white pants or a white shirt, that coat doesn’t stay immaculate for long, at least not without a great deal of hard work and foresight.
You’ll need to groom these dogs frequently. That means brushing them every day and bathing them frequently. They can get baths as often as twice a week, and they should never go longer than 6 weeks without a dip in the tub.
They’re prone to tear staining around their eyes, so you should wipe their eyes frequently to prevent that from happening.
Besides that, they also need their nails trimmed every few weeks and their teeth brushed daily.

In general, these dogs are fairly similar regardless of sex, and either male or female Eskimo dogs can make a wonderful addition to your pack.

Several traveling circuses in the early 20th century used American Eskimos in their acts. The Cooper Brothers’ Railroad Circus is believed to be the first to utilize their talents in 1917, and Barnum & Bailey famously had an American Eskimo that was capable of walking a tightrope.
Their motives weren’t purely to entertain, however. These circuses also sold puppies after the show, hoping to cash in on members of the public who had just been mesmerized by these dogs’ skills.
Sadly, we can only imagine how many impulsively purchased American Eskimo puppies were later mistreated or abandoned when it was discovered that they weren’t inherently capable of performing circus tricks. Then again, it’s believed that many of today’s American Eskimos can thank those circus dogs for their existence, so maybe it wasn’t such a bad thing after all.
The three sizes are toy, miniature, and standard, and these are accurate descriptions of the dogs. At just 40 pounds, standard Eskimos aren’t big dogs, except when you compare them to toy and miniature Eskimos, which tip the scales at 10 and 20 pounds, respectively.
Beyond the size disparity, there isn’t much difference between the three types of dogs, so you should get the full American Eskimo experience (for better or worse) regardless of which size you bring home.
American Eskimo dogs are descended from German Spitz dogs, and that’s what they were originally called when they made their debut on American shores.
However, after WWI, describing anything as “German” was a sure way to torpedo their popularity stateside (and this situation wouldn’t exactly improve in the decades that followed). Devotees of the German Spitz then changed their name to the American Spitz, and over time, that transitioned to the American Eskimo (and the Miniature American Eskimo).

If you want a dog that’s smart, loving, loyal, and just the right amount of mischievous, the American Eskimo is worthy of your consideration. These dogs have big personalities and they’ll quickly steal your heart.
Be prepared to spend a ton of your free time exercising and grooming them, though, because while they have low-maintenance personalities, their bodies require a great deal of upkeep (hey, it takes hard work to look this good).
